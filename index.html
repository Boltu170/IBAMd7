📚Knowledge Box — Market FailuresRead me first! ▼

🏭 Monopoly — no competition

One firm dominates → no pressure to improve or lower prices. Can arise naturally (e.g. water pipes) or through competition (e.g. Google). Government responds with regulation, break-up, or nationalisation.

⚠️ Temporary monopolies can be fine — rewarding innovation. The problem is permanent, entrenched power.

🌐 Public Goods — the free rider problem

Public goods are non-rival (one person's use doesn't reduce availability) AND non-excludable (non-payers can't be blocked). No private firm will supply them → government must.

💡 "Public good" is a technical term. Healthcare is NOT one — it's rival and excludable.

💨 Externalities — third-party spillovers

A transaction affects people outside the deal. Negative (e.g. pollution) → market overproduces. Positive (e.g. vaccines) → market underproduces. Fix: tax negatives, subsidise positives.

📚Knowledge Box — Rival, Excludable & Public GoodsRead me first! ▼
Rival + Excludable → Private good (sandwich, car)
Non-rival + Excludable → Club good (Netflix, gym)
Rival + Non-excludable → Common resource (ocean fish)
Non-rival + Non-excludable → Public good (street lighting)

Rival = one person's use reduces what's left for others. Excludable = non-payers can be blocked.

⚠️ Pure public goods are rare — most real goods (roads, healthcare) sit somewhere in between.
💡 Free rider problem: if you can't exclude non-payers, no private firm will supply the good → government steps in.

📚Knowledge Box — Externalities & Complex RealityRead me first! ▼

🔴 Negative Externalities → market overproduces

Producer doesn't bear the full social cost. Examples: factory pollution, carbon emissions, noise, second-hand smoke. Fix: tax or regulate.

🟢 Positive Externalities → market underproduces

Producer doesn't capture the full social benefit. Examples: vaccines (herd immunity), education, R&D spillovers, beekeeping. Fix: subsidise or provide directly.

🧩 Complex Reality

Coase: if property rights are clear and transaction costs are low, parties can negotiate without government. In practice this rarely holds. Power imbalances, asymmetric information and bounded rationality all complicate things.

⚠️ Almost everything has some externality. Intervene only when the benefit outweighs the cost of intervention.

📚Knowledge Box — Price Elasticity of Demand & Bounded RationalityRead me first! ▼

📐 Price Elasticity of Demand (PED)

PED = % Δ quantity ÷ % Δ price
|PED| < 1 → Inelastic (qty changes less than price)
|PED| > 1 → Elastic (qty changes more than price)

% Δ Qty = PED × % Δ Price (opposite direction)
e.g. PED 2.0, price +10% → qty −20%
⚠️ PED is technically negative, but expressed as a positive. Qty always moves in the OPPOSITE direction to price.

🧠 Bounded Rationality — how firms exploit it

Consumers have limited time and information. Tactics used against them:

  • Anchoring — high "original" price makes sale price look cheap
  • Time pressure — "Lightning deals", countdowns create urgency
  • Social proof — star ratings replace independent research
  • Framing — "70% off" rather than the actual £ saving
💡 Branding, switching costs and shrinkflation can reduce PED — consumers pay more without noticing.
